The Arc
of Art

From the breathtaking mastery of the Renaissance to the deliberate chaos of Modernism — what happened, why, and whether anything truly went wrong.

↓ SCROLL TO EXPLORE ↓

"Why is Renaissance art so impossibly beautiful — and why do we call ugly things art today?"

This is one of the most honest and important questions you can ask about human culture. The answer involves philosophy, religion, capitalism, war, photography, ego, and a fundamental transformation in what human beings believed art was for.

It is a story spanning 600 years — of extraordinary beauty being built, refined, then deliberately demolished. Some of that demolition was genius. Some was fraud. Understanding which is which is the beginning of wisdom about art.

I

The Renaissance

1400s – 1600s · ITALY → EUROPE · THE SUMMIT OF SKILL

The Renaissance — meaning rebirth — was Europe waking up from a thousand years of medieval flatness and rediscovering the ancient Greek and Roman conviction that the human body, the natural world, and mathematical proportion were sacred and worth depicting with total fidelity.

Medieval art had been deliberately two-dimensional and symbolic. A figure's size indicated spiritual importance, not physical reality. Gold backgrounds replaced actual skies. The goal was to point toward God, not to recreate the world He made. Beautiful in its own way — but a different purpose entirely.

Then, in Florence in the early 1400s, something cracked open. Brunelleschi worked out the mathematics of linear perspective. Masaccio used it immediately in frescoes that made walls dissolve into three-dimensional space. The race had begun: to make the painting a window so convincing you'd reach through it.

The goal was singular and shared across every artist, patron, and viewer: make the painting indistinguishable from looking at the real world through glass.

This was not easy. It required mastering an entire science of vision, light, anatomy, and geometry. Artists trained for 10–20 years under master craftsmen — grinding pigments, stretching canvases, copying drawings — before touching an important surface. Leonardo da Vinci spent years dissecting human corpses to understand how muscles moved beneath skin. Michelangelo could sculpt a marble figure so accurate that viewers wept, believing it might breathe.

THE FOUR PILLARS OF RENAISSANCE MASTERY

Chiaroscuro The dramatic graduation from light into deep shadow, giving figures weight, volume, and the illusion of three dimensions on a flat surface.
Linear Perspective Brunelleschi's mathematical system: parallel lines converge at a "vanishing point," creating the optical illusion of infinite depth.
Sfumato Leonardo's invention — blending edges so gradually they become invisible, like the haze of real atmosphere. The Mona Lisa's smile is sfumato.
Contrapposto Figures posed with weight shifted to one leg, shoulders and hips counter-rotating — the natural stance of a living body, stolen from Greek sculpture.

Equally important: the artists were servants. They painted for the Church, for princes, for the Medici banking dynasty. Patrons dictated subject, size, color, and number of figures. There was no room for "personal expression." You painted what was ordered, and you painted it as flawlessly as God-given talent and decades of discipline would allow. The result was collective excellence pointed at a shared vision of beauty.

THE ANNUNCIATION — Fra Angelico Style, c.1440
Observe the converging floor tiles drawing the eye into depth — pure linear perspective. The angel's wings are anatomically articulated. The loggia opens onto a real Tuscan landscape. Every element is in service of making you believe you are witnessing an actual moment.
SFUMATO PORTRAIT — Leonardo Style, c.1503–1519
Notice how there are no hard edges anywhere on the face — every boundary dissolves into the next tone. This is sfumato. The eyes hold an expression impossible to name. The background landscape melts into atmospheric haze. Five hundred years later, no one has fully replicated it.

The Details that Make Renaissance Art Extraordinary

ANATOMICAL HAND STUDY
Renaissance masters dissected corpses to understand every tendon and bone. These were not practice sketches — they were scientific documents of the human machine.
DRAPERY FOLD STUDY
Apprentices spent months painting nothing but cloth — studying how fabric falls, gathers, and casts shadows. This mastery is why Renaissance robes look so impossibly real.
LINEAR PERSPECTIVE STUDY
All lines converge at the "vanishing point" — a mathematical invention that creates the illusion of infinite depth on a flat surface. This was one of the greatest intellectual breakthroughs of the 15th century.
II

The Baroque

1600s – 1750s · CARAVAGGIO · REMBRANDT · VERMEER

Baroque artists inherited all the technical mastery of the Renaissance — and turned the dial up to maximum emotional intensity. Where Renaissance painting aimed for calm, ordered beauty, Baroque painting aimed to grab you by the throat.

Caravaggio is the revolutionary figure. He would hire real working people — prostitutes, thieves, murderers — as models for sacred scenes. His Virgin Mary lay dead like a real woman, swollen and pale. His apostles had dirty feet and calloused hands. The Church rejected some works as blasphemous. But those who understood saw something new: sacred events happening to real humans, not gilded symbols.

His lighting — a single source from one direction, cutting through absolute darkness — was theatrical in a way the world had never seen. Figures seem to materialize from nothing.

Baroque is still breathtakingly beautiful. The technique remains Renaissance-level masterful. What changed was the emotional temperature — from serene contemplation to raw, urgent drama.

Rembrandt van Rijn pushed portraiture to its ultimate limits. He painted himself over 90 times across his life — a psychological autobiography in paint. He could render the sheen of silk, the roughness of velvet, the translucency of aged skin in the same painting with equal mastery. His late self-portraits, showing a man broken by bankruptcy and loss, are among the most psychologically honest images ever made.

Vermeer painted domestic interiors — women reading letters, making lace, pouring milk — with a stillness and light quality so perfect that 20th-century scientists discovered he likely used a camera obscura to project the scene before painting it. Still extraordinary. The technique was uncompromised.

THE CALLING OF ST. MATTHEW — Caravaggio Style, c.1600
Tenebrism at its most extreme — figures sculpted entirely by a single light source against pure blackness. The technique was revolutionary. Notice the third figure barely visible in shadow. Caravaggio didn't blend light into dark — he cut between them like a knife.
SELF-PORTRAIT — Rembrandt Style, c.1660s
Rembrandt painted himself over 90 times — a lifelong psychological diary. Notice the impasto texture, the warm amber light, the unflinching record of aging. This is not idealization. This is truth. His late portraits are among the most emotionally honest images in human history.

The Great Disruption

Six forces collided in the 19th century and shattered the 400-year tradition of painted beauty

Photography (1839)

When the daguerreotype appeared, it could capture a human face more accurately than ten years of painting training. The existential question was immediate: if a machine can perfectly reproduce reality, what is the purpose of a painter? Every subsequent art movement is an attempt to answer this.

The Industrial Revolution

Cities became grey, mechanical, and inhuman. The Romantics fled to nature and emotion in protest. Later, modernists embraced the machine aesthetic. Either way, the serene world of Renaissance painting felt remote and dishonest. Artists wanted to paint how the new world felt — fractured, fast, ugly, electric.

Loss of Religious Patronage

The Church had funded art for centuries with a clear directive: glorify God. As secularism rose, this shared mission collapsed. Artists became free — but free to do what? Without an agreed purpose, art splintered into hundreds of competing private visions. There was no longer any shared standard of success.

World War I (1914–1918)

Twenty million dead in industrial-scale slaughter. Classical beauty now felt like a lie — a prettified mask over the real face of civilization, which had just shown itself capable of factory-scale murder. Artists like the Dadaists responded with deliberate ugliness and absurdity as acts of moral honesty. If culture produced this, then culture must be destroyed.

Freud & The Unconscious

Freud's discovery that beneath our rational surface lurked irrational dreams, desires, and fears gave artists permission to bypass rational beauty entirely. The Surrealists painted the unconscious — impossible dreamscapes that made no visual logic but felt psychologically truer than any realistic scene could.

Philosophy Changes Everything

Hegel argued art's purpose was to express Spirit and Ideas, not to imitate nature. Nietzsche declared old values dead. By the early 20th century, the art world had accepted that skill in representation was philosophically irrelevant — what mattered was the concept, the statement, the provocation.

III

Impressionism — The First Crack

1860s – 1890s · MONET · RENOIR · DEGAS · PARIS

In 1874, a group of painters rejected from the official Paris Salon held their own exhibition. A critic mocked Monet's painting Impression, Sunrise with the word "impressionist" — intending it as an insult. It became the movement's name.

The Impressionists were NOT abandoning skill. They were making a sophisticated visual argument: the human eye does not actually see sharp edges and flat areas of color. It sees light, in constant flux, in patches of color that the brain assembles into objects. So painting those patches directly was actually more accurate than the painstaking academic rendering.

Monet would set up multiple canvases before the same haystack or cathedral and paint them at different hours of day, capturing how light transformed the same object into something unrecognizable by afternoon. He spent thirty years painting the water lily pond at his garden in Giverny — not because he couldn't paint anything else, but because the study of light on water was an infinite subject.

This was still mastery — but mastery of a different, more subjective truth. The shift was from "what does the world look like?" to "how does the world appear to my eye?"

Degas painted ballet dancers obsessively — not in posed grace but in the unglamorous moments: warming up, adjusting a shoe, caught in an awkward stance. He used photography's cropped, accidental compositions. He painted what the eye actually catches, not what the mind composes. Still extraordinarily beautiful. But the subject of painting had quietly shifted from the world to the act of perception itself.

WATER LILIES — Monet Style, c.1906
No hard edges exist anywhere. The pond surface, the lily pads, the flower reflections, the sky — all dissolve into each other through broken brushstrokes of pure color. Monet spent 30 years painting this pond. The closer you stand, the more abstract it becomes. Step back, and reality assembles itself.
BALLET DANCER — Degas Style, c.1880
Degas used photography's accidental cropping — a dancer mid-gesture, background figures blurred and incomplete. The stage floor recedes in perspective. The tutu layers dissolve into impressionist touch. Still technically brilliant — but capturing a fleeting moment, not a composed ideal.
IV

Post-Impressionism — Art Goes Inward

1886–1910s · VAN GOGH · CÉZANNE · GAUGUIN

Three giants pulled in three different directions, and together they broke open the door to everything that followed.

Vincent van Gogh — Emotion as Color

Van Gogh had been a preacher before he was a painter. He believed painting should convey the spiritual essence of things — not their surface appearance. His swirling skies, his pulsating stars, his writhing cypress trees weren't what those things looked like. They were what they felt like to a man of overwhelming, almost unbearable feeling. He sold one painting in his lifetime and died poor. Within twenty years of his death, he was recognized as one of the greatest painters who ever lived.

Paul Cézanne — Structure Over Surface

Cézanne wanted to paint what lay beneath the surface shimmer of Impressionist color — the geometric bones of the world. His apples aren't round; they're arrangements of planes that suggest roundness. His Mont Sainte-Victoire mountain is simultaneously flat and deep. He wrote: "treat nature by the cylinder, the sphere, the cone." Picasso called him "the father of us all." Without Cézanne, there is no Cubism.

Paul Gauguin — Primitivism & Escape

Gauguin fled to Tahiti to escape what he saw as the moral corruption of European civilization. His flat, bold colors and simplified forms borrowed from non-Western art — Polynesian, Japanese woodblock prints, Egyptian friezes. He was rejecting the entire European tradition of three-dimensional illusionism in favor of something more primal and direct.

Together, these three men established a new principle: art's purpose is not to show what the world looks like, but to reveal how the artist experiences and understands it. This shift, once made, could not be unmade.

THE STARRY NIGHT — Van Gogh Style, 1889
The sky doesn't look like this. Stars don't have visible halos. The sky doesn't swirl in great turbulent eddies. But this is how Van Gogh FELT the night — alive, enormous, churning with a beauty so overwhelming it was almost terrifying. He painted this from a psychiatric hospital. He'd cut off his ear months earlier.
MONT SAINTE-VICTOIRE — Cézanne Style, c.1902
Cézanne painted this mountain over 60 times. Notice how the mountain is made of flat geometric planes, not blended tone. The sky, fields, and mountain are all equal — there's no hierarchy of foreground and background. He was looking for the structure beneath surface appearance. Picasso called him "the father of us all."
V

Cubism & Abstraction — Art Becomes Ideas

1907 – 1930s · PICASSO · BRAQUE · KANDINSKY · MONDRIAN

Picasso and Braque invented Cubism together in 1907–1914 — one of the most radical breaks in art history. Starting from Cézanne's geometric planes, they asked: why should a painting show only one view of an object? The mind knows a face has two eyes even when one is hidden in profile. A table exists in three dimensions even in a two-dimensional painting. Why not show all of it simultaneously?

The result looks "wrong" to the eye trained on Renaissance perspective — because it deliberately is. Cubism shows multiple viewpoints at once, collapsing time and angle into a single image. It is analytical, not emotional. The mind, not the eye, is the viewer's instrument.

Then Wassily Kandinsky went further still: he painted with no subject at all. Pure color, pure shape, pure line — with no reference to any external reality. He believed colors and forms had direct emotional equivalents, the way musical notes do. Blue = spiritual. Yellow = aggressive. A painting could move you without depicting anything recognizable, the way a symphony moves you without telling a story.

This was the decisive break. Once Kandinsky proved you could make a painting about nothing but the relationships between shapes and colors — and still have it mean something — the door to everything was open. Art had fully become a medium for pure ideas.

Mondrian took abstraction to its logical extreme: grids of black lines with primary color rectangles. No curves. No reference to nature. Pure rational order. He called it "Neoplasticism" and believed it expressed the universal harmony beneath surface reality. Many people look at a Mondrian and see decoration. He believed he was painting the fundamental structure of existence.

WEEPING WOMAN — Picasso Style, 1937
The face is shown from multiple angles simultaneously — front, profile, and three-quarter. Eyes displaced. Tears rendered as geometric planes of blue. This woman is not observed; she is analyzed, dissected, and reassembled according to emotional logic, not visual logic. Picasso said: "I paint forms as I think them, not as I see them."
COMPOSITION — Kandinsky Style, c.1913
No subject. No recognizable objects. Pure color and form as direct emotional communication — like music. Kandinsky believed blue evoked spiritual depth, yellow aggressive energy, red passion, black finality. The painting is a symphony for the eye. This was the first truly abstract painting in Western art history.
VI

Dada & Surrealism — The Dream & The Bomb

1916 – 1940s · DUCHAMP · DALÍ · MAGRITTE · ERNST

World War I killed nearly 20 million people using the products of European civilization — railways, chemistry, engineering, guns. A generation of artists concluded that if civilization produced this, then civilization must be destroyed — including its art.

Dada (the name was chosen from a dictionary at random — deliberately meaningless) was born in Zürich in 1916. Its most famous act: Marcel Duchamp took a factory-produced urinal, signed it "R. Mutt, 1917," titled it Fountain, and submitted it to an art exhibition as a work of art. The exhibition committee refused it. Duchamp's point was made: the art object has no inherent value. What makes it art is the decision to call it art, and the context in which it is placed.

This single gesture changed art forever. After Duchamp, any object in any context could be art if an artist declared it so. Skill became entirely optional. The concept was everything.

Surrealism, by contrast, was not nihilistic. It was genuinely trying to access a deeper truth — the truth of the unconscious mind, which Freud had revealed was stranger and more real than waking reason.

Salvador Dalí had the technical skill of the Renaissance masters — he could paint silk, glass, and skin with photographic precision. He used that skill to depict impossible things: melting watches, elephants on spider legs, women made of drawers. The technical mastery was deployed in service of dream logic. Magritte painted an extremely realistic pipe and wrote beneath it "This is not a pipe" — drawing attention to the gap between representation and reality. These weren't failures of skill. They were philosophy conducted in paint.

THE PERSISTENCE OF MEMORY — Dalí Style, 1931
Painted with photographic precision — the sky, sand, cliff, shadows are all meticulously observed. Only the watches are impossible, melting like soft cheese in a desert heat. This combination of hyper-realism and dream-logic is Surrealism's great trick: make the impossible feel like a memory you actually have. Dalí said he painted "hand-painted dream photographs."
THE SON OF MAN — Magritte Style, 1964
Magritte painted himself in a bowler hat with an apple floating before his face. He wrote: "Everything we see hides another thing. We always want to see what is hidden by what we see." The painting is philosophically precise — and demands you think, not just feel. His great pipe painting bore the text: "This is not a pipe." It isn't. It's a picture of a pipe.
VII

Abstract Expressionism & Pop Art

1940s – 1970s · POLLOCK · ROTHKO · WARHOL · LICHTENSTEIN

After World War II, the center of the art world shifted from Paris to New York. The Americans brought a new energy — larger, louder, more physical.

Jackson Pollock laid his canvases on the floor and poured, dripped, and flung paint across them while walking around the edges. He was influenced by Jungian psychology and Native American sand painting — the idea that the act of creating was itself the artwork. The finished painting was a record of his body moving through space. He called it "action painting." When critics asked how he knew when a painting was finished, he said: "I know."

Mark Rothko painted large rectangles of luminous color, floating against each other — no imagery, no narrative. But stand before a large Rothko for five minutes and something happens: the colors begin to breathe, to pulse, to draw you in. People have reported weeping before them without knowing why. Rothko believed he was painting "basic human emotions — tragedy, ecstasy, doom." He refused to let his paintings hang in restaurants. He said they required contemplation, not decoration.

Then Andy Warhol turned it all inside out. He printed a Campbell's soup can. He silk-screened Marilyn Monroe's face in garish colors, repeated 50 times. He said: "I want to be a machine." Pop Art demolished the boundary between "high" art and commercial culture — and in doing so, asked the most uncomfortable question: was there ever a real difference?

Warhol understood that in a mass-media, consumer society, the truly radical act was not to flee commerce but to mirror it back with deadpan precision until you couldn't tell worship from critique. His factory produced art like a factory. It was genius or cynicism or both.

ACTION PAINTING — Pollock Style, c.1950
The canvas was on the floor. Pollock walked around it, dripping and flinging paint with sticks, hardened brushes, and cans. No brushstroke touched the canvas. The painting records the physical energy of his moving body. Critics called it chaos. Pollock called it "pure harmony." Scientists later found his drip patterns follow fractal geometry found in nature.
MARILYN DIPTYCH — Warhol Style, 1962
Warhol made 50 silk-screen reproductions of the same publicity photo of Marilyn Monroe, made just after her death. Left half: bright, garish colors — the celebrity machine. Right half: fading into grey — the human being disappearing. It looks like a factory product. It IS a factory product. That's precisely the point.

The Same Subject — 500 Years Apart

A human face. The most painted subject in history. Here is what changed.

Renaissance Portrait — c.1480

Sfumato blending, anatomical precision, psychological depth through realistic rendering. The goal: make you believe a real person is before you.

⟷

Cubist Portrait — c.1937

Multiple viewpoints simultaneously. Geometric deconstruction. The face is analyzed, not observed. The goal: show how a mind knows a face, not how an eye sees it.

Light — Then and Now

Woman Reading a Letter — Vermeer Style, c.1663

Natural window light modeled on the figure with extraordinary subtlety. The entire painting is about the quality of morning light. Technically flawless. Emotionally hushed.

⟷

Color Field Painting — Rothko Style, c.1961

No object, no figure, no narrative. Three rectangles of red. Stand before a large Rothko for five minutes — the colors begin to breathe. People have wept without knowing why. This IS light. Just not observed light.

Every child is an artist. The problem is how to remain an artist once we grow up.
— Pablo Picasso

The Full Arc — At a Glance

1400s–1600s

Renaissance — The Summit of Skill

Masaccio, Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael. Perfect command of light, anatomy, perspective. Shared purpose: honor God and capture reality. An apprentice trains 15 years before touching a major surface. Art is collective, disciplined, and transcendently beautiful.

PEAK SHARED BEAUTY
1600s–1750s

Baroque & Rococo — Drama, then Decoration

Caravaggio's tenebrism shocks the Church with dirty-footed apostles. Rembrandt paints the psychology of aging. Vermeer captures morning light with impossible subtlety. Then Rococo frilliness signals aristocratic decadence. Still masterful throughout.

MASTERFUL · INTENSE
1839

Photography — The Crisis Begins

A machine reproduces reality in seconds. Painters must find a new purpose. Every subsequent art movement is an answer to the question: what can painting do that photography cannot?

THE GREAT DISRUPTION
1860s–1890s

Impressionism — How Light Feels

Monet, Renoir, Degas, Pissarro. Rejected from the official Salon. Paint how the eye actually perceives light — in patches and flickers, not sharp outlines. Scandalous in 1874. Now the most beloved paintings in the world.

BEAUTIFUL · SUBJECTIVE
1886–1910s

Post-Impressionism — Art Goes Inward

Van Gogh paints emotional truth. Cézanne paints geometric structure. Gauguin paints primitivist escape. The door to abstraction opens a crack. Individually brilliant; collectively, they broke art's connection to optical reality forever.

GENIUS · INCREASINGLY PERSONAL
1907–1930s

Cubism & Abstraction — Painting Ideas

Picasso and Braque: multiple viewpoints simultaneously. Kandinsky: pure abstract emotion with no subject at all. Mondrian: perfect grids. Art is now fully conceptual — about ideas of seeing, not seeing itself.

BREAKS FROM TRADITION
1916–1923

Dada — Art as Philosophical Bomb

Post-WWI nihilism. Duchamp submits a urinal as "Fountain." The point: the art world is absurd, civilization is absurd, therefore art must be absurd. NOT incompetence — deliberate, devastating provocation that changed what "art" could mean.

INTENTIONAL DECONSTRUCTION
1920s–1940s

Surrealism — The Dream World

Dalí, Magritte, Ernst, Kahlo. Access the unconscious mind. Hyper-realistic technique in service of impossible imagery. Dream logic rather than visual logic. Often technically brilliant — but depicting the impossible made to look real.

DEEPLY CONCEPTUAL · OFTEN BEAUTIFUL
1940s–1960s

Abstract Expressionism & Pop Art

Pollock drips paint from the floor — the gesture IS the art. Rothko's color fields move people to tears with no imagery. Warhol prints soup cans — commerce as art, art as commerce. Skill at rendering is now entirely optional.

CONCEPT OVER CRAFT
1960s–Today

Contemporary Art — Anything, Anyone, Anywhere

Damien Hirst: shark in formaldehyde. Banksy: shreds his own painting at auction. NFTs. AI-generated images. Conceptual installations. The question "Is this art?" IS the artwork. The art market rewards novelty, brand, and critical endorsement over craft.

THE OPEN QUESTION

So — Did Something Go Wrong?

This is the most important question — and it deserves an honest answer, not a diplomatic one.

The Renaissance was beautiful because four things aligned perfectly: a clear shared purpose (honor God and capture reality), an agreed standard of excellence (anatomical precision, perspective, chiaroscuro — all objectively measurable), a rigorous apprenticeship tradition that produced genuine mastery, and a public audience that could understand and judge what it was looking at. When you saw a Raphael, you didn't need a critic to explain it. You could see that it was extraordinary.

The Renaissance artist asked: "How can I paint this as perfectly as possible?"

The modern artist asks: "What is the idea I want to express — and does painting it even serve that idea?"

These are not the same question. Neither is objectively wrong. But the shift had real costs that the art world has been reluctant to acknowledge honestly.

What Was Lost

The shared language of beauty. The sense that art should reward the viewer's gaze with something beyond what ordinary life provides. The dignity of craft — the idea that the ability to make something extraordinary by hand, after years of discipline, is itself a form of honor. And most importantly: the connection between artist and audience. When most people enter a contemporary art gallery and feel confused, alienated, or vaguely fraudulent, the art world typically diagnoses this as the audience's failure to understand. This is a strange and arrogant position for what is supposed to be a communication medium.

What Was Gained

Art that challenges comfortable assumptions. Art that doesn't beautify suffering — that can represent the horror of war (Picasso's Guernica), the terror of the unconscious (Dalí), the violence of history (Goya). Art that experiments with new visual languages that have no precedent. The freedom to make work about anything — not just what a Church or king approves. Some of this is genuinely important. Kandinsky's discovery that pure abstraction could move people emotionally is as significant as any Renaissance breakthrough. Duchamp's philosophical provocation genuinely expanded what art could mean.

What Genuinely Went Wrong

The art market. When collectors, auction houses, and galleries began paying astronomical sums for conceptual work, the system became one where the story around the art — who made it, what critic endorsed it, which collection it came from — became more valuable than the object. This rewarded clever self-promotion as much as genuine vision. An artist with an excellent publicist and the right social connections could place fundamentally empty work in major institutions. Since there is no longer any agreed standard of excellence to measure against, no one can prove the emperor has no clothes — and saying so merely marks you as unsophisticated.

Picasso's Cubism looked ugly and chaotic to 1910 audiences. Now we see its structural genius. Some contemporary art that looks meaningless today will be understood as visionary in 100 years. But not all of it — and possibly not most of it. The art world currently has no reliable filter for which is which. That is the real problem.

The Question Behind Your Question

You are not wrong to love Renaissance art deeply and feel alienated by much contemporary work. That is not ignorance — it is a legitimate aesthetic and philosophical position that many serious thinkers share, including artists. Roger Scruton, the philosopher, spent years making this argument. Tom Wolfe wrote a devastating satirical book about the contemporary art world called The Painted Word, arguing that modern art had become incomprehensible without the accompanying text of critical theory — that the theory had replaced the art.

What's worth understanding is why the shift happened — not to accept everything that followed as equally valid, but to see clearly which modern work genuinely grapples with something real, and which is dressed-up emptiness given a philosophical veneer after the fact.

The Renaissance masterworks are extraordinary because the people who made them had devoted their entire lives to the discipline of making them. They earned their beauty through decades of effort. The question to ask of any work of art, old or new, is still the same one: Did someone put themselves completely into this? Does it offer me something I could not have found without it?

Sometimes the answer is yes, even for a Rothko rectangle. Often it is no, for a shark in formaldehyde. Trust your eye, your feeling, and your judgment. The history of art gives you more than enough material to calibrate them.
