On the Series

Beauty from
the Mundane
& Ordinary

I–XVIII

This series began without intention. The route between ICTS and NCBS — two research institutes on opposite ends of Bengaluru — takes roughly forty minutes by bus. I began photographing through the window to pass the time. I have not stopped.

"Each photograph captures a moment. No matter whether it's blurry or in focus, it reveals a depth beneath the surface — layers beneath what was considered ordinary."

Here in these streets, life unfolds in all of its nuances. A vendor mid-gesture. A tree pushing through concrete. A figure caught between one step and the next. These are not exceptional moments — that is precisely the point. The series is a sustained argument that beauty can arise from the mundane and the ordinary, that the unremarkable street, seen with enough attention and patience, contains depths that reward looking.

The blurriness is not failure. Blur is what motion looks like. An out-of-focus image is not a mistake — it is what it means to be moving through a moving city, trying to fix one moment in a stream of moments that have no intention of stopping for you. I have come to prefer the images where the camera's mechanical eye and the city's indifference to being watched produce something together: a collaboration between intention and accident.

The name of the parent blog — Studies of Natural History — is not accidental. These are field notes. The specimen is Bengaluru. The observer is in motion. Black and white removes the distraction of colour and asks: what is actually happening here? What is the quality of the light? The posture of the body? The relationship between this person and this street, right now, at this speed?

Each archive is one journey. The photographs are numbered but not curated — they are a chronology, not a selection. The discipline is entirely in the shooting.

Layers Beneath
the Ordinary

NCBS to ICTS · 21.02.2026
Archive 7 was the point at which the series declared itself. Until then, the photographs were accumulating without a name for what they were doing. Here, writing about this archive, the philosophy of the whole project became clear: each photograph captures a moment — no matter whether blurry or in focus, it reveals a depth beneath the surface, layers beneath what was considered ordinary.

Here in these streets, life unfolds in all its nuances. Not the Bengaluru of brochures or tech-campus mythology — the Bengaluru of the actual street, which has been unfolding this way for decades before any camera arrived to notice it. The series is a sustained act of noticing. Beauty is not imported into the mundane. It is uncovered there.

Archive XV

The Crossing

ICTS, Bengaluru · 01.03.2026
The bus held at this crossing long enough to compose. It is the closest I have come to a planned photograph in this series — everything else is pure reaction. What I notice in retrospect is how the geometry arranged itself without my instruction: the vertical of the tree, the horizontal of the road marking, the diagonal implied by the figures' movement. The city provided the composition. I merely decided when.

Midday Dust

ICTS, Bengaluru · 05.03.2026
Shot at a signal stop — the rare moment when the city holds its breath between movements. Midday light through dust and glass flattens everything into near-equal grey tones. No hierarchy of subject. The street and the sky are equally present, which is the truth about how Bengaluru looks at noon: everything bleached to the same luminous grey.

Threshold &
Dirty Glass

ICTS to NCBS · 05.03.2026
Left: A figure at the edge of the frame — partly seen, partly lost to motion blur. The indeterminacy is the point. Most street photography tries to capture the subject fully. I am interested in the threshold between presence and absence: the moment before the bus carries the figure out of frame, and the subject is simultaneously here and already gone.

Right: Light fracturing through the glass at an angle that turns an ordinary street into something geological — layers of grey, like strata. The bus window was dirty. I did not clean it. The grime is part of the image, part of the honesty of the method.

Archive XVI

Garden City,
Surviving

NCBS to ICTS · 03.03.2026
Bengaluru was once called the Garden City. Trees surviving between concrete are what remains of that description — insistent green things pushing through wherever they are permitted to persist. I am drawn to this tension in the city: the organic and the built, neither winning. In black and white, the tree and the building become equally grey, equally substantial. The photograph refuses to decide which matters more.

Perception
at Speed

NCBS to ICTS · 26.02.2026
Motion blur at the edges of the frame, the centre briefly sharp. This is how the eye actually works on a moving bus — you fix on one point and everything else smears into peripheral suggestion. I am interested in honest rendering of perception, not the camera's mechanical freezing of it. The photographs that interest me most are those where the blur is not a flaw to be corrected but information about the experience of seeing.

Archive XII

The Route
Repeats

ICTS to NCBS · 25.02.2026
Repetition is one of the things the bus ride teaches. The same buildings pass every day — I have photographed this stretch of road perhaps eighteen times now. But the light never repeats. What the series is really about, I think, is the impossibility of the same moment twice: the route is identical, the variations infinite.

Repetition is also how the naturalist works. The field notebook fills with the same specimen observed across seasons. The accumulation of similar observations is how patterns emerge. Each archive is one observation. The series is the study.

The City,
Unguarded

ICTS, Bengaluru · 05.03.2026
This is the photograph I return to most often from the recent archives. Something about the distribution of figures in the frame — their spacing, the way they occupy the street without relating to each other — feels true to how cities actually work. The loneliness is structural, not personal. Everyone is simply going somewhere. The camera happened to be there.
