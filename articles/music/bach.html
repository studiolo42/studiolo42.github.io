Bach & the Architecture
of Counterpoint

How one composer turned mathematical structure into the most moving music ever written — and why the Well-Tempered Clavier is the Bible of Western music.

Era Baroque · 1685–1750

There is a famous story — almost certainly apocryphal, but too perfect to discard — about the philosopher Friedrich Nietzsche sitting at a piano in Turin, shortly before his mental collapse, playing Bach over and over and weeping. Whether or not it happened, it captures something true: Bach has always had the capacity to undo people, to produce in listeners a quality of emotion that seems to exceed what mere notes should be capable of.

The paradox of Bach is that his music is, on its surface, a formal exercise. Counterpoint — the practice of weaving independent melodic lines together so that they interact according to strict rules — is a technical discipline. To study it is to study grammar. And yet in Bach's hands, this grammar produces something that sounds less like craft than like revelation.

What Counterpoint Actually Is

Most people experience music as melody plus accompaniment: a tune on top, chords supporting it below. This is the model of most Western popular music, most opera, most Romantic orchestral writing. The melody carries the meaning. The harmony supports it.

Counterpoint is something else entirely. In contrapuntal music, every voice is melodically independent. The bass is not just supporting the soprano — it is singing its own tune, which happens to be in dialogue with the soprano's tune in ways governed by rules of consonance and dissonance. When two or three or four of these independent voices weave together and produce, at every moment, something that is harmonically coherent and emotionally unified — that is what counterpoint achieves.

The Fugue

The fugue is counterpoint's highest form. It begins with a single melodic idea — the subject — stated alone in one voice. A second voice then enters with the same subject while the first continues with new material called the countersubject. Then a third voice enters, and a fourth. The subject returns throughout in different keys and different voices. A Bach fugue is, in a literal sense, a conversation between independent minds who share a common obsession.

Here is roughly how it looks when written out — four voices entering in succession, each bringing the same subject into different registers:

SOPRANO: SUBJECT ──────────── COUNTERSUBJECT ──── FREE VOICE ─ ALTO: ─────────────────── SUBJECT ──────────── COUNTERSUBJECT ──── TENOR: ─────────────────── ───────────────────── SUBJECT ──────────── BASS: ─────────────────── ───────────────────── ─────────── SUBJECT ────

What that diagram cannot show is what it sounds like when it works. It sounds inevitable — as though the music could not have been any other way, as though these four voices had always been moving toward this convergence and the composer simply found the point at which they arrived.

The Well-Tempered Clavier

Between 1722 and 1742, Bach wrote forty-eight preludes and fugues — two in every possible major and minor key, collected in two books now known as the Well-Tempered Clavier. It was, on one level, a pedagogical work: a systematic demonstration that music could be written in all twenty-four keys. The title referred to a tuning system that made this possible by distributing a small mathematical compromise equally across all intervals.

But calling it a pedagogical work is like calling the Goldberg Variations a set of exercises. The Well-Tempered Clavier is a universe. Each prelude establishes a mood, a texture, a world. Each fugue then develops within that world with three, four, or five voices in conversation. Some are severe and austere. Some are almost playful. Some achieve a quality of grief that does not seem explicable by music theory alone.

"The Well-Tempered Clavier is not just a collection of pieces. It is a demonstration that the whole world of human feeling can be expressed within a single formal constraint — and that the constraint, far from limiting, is what makes the expression possible."

The C Minor Prelude

Take the Prelude in C minor from Book I. It is not technically difficult — a keyboard student of intermediate ability can play it. It consists of a single repeated rhythmic pattern in the right hand against a moving bass. And yet it produces, in almost everyone who hears it, something close to desolation. The weight of the key, the slow descent of the bass line, the relentless forward motion of the rhythm: these combine to make something that feels like fate.

No one can fully explain why. That is what makes it music rather than mathematics.

The Mathematical and the Human

Bach was a deeply religious man — a Lutheran who wrote Soli Deo Gloria (To God alone the glory) on his manuscripts. He believed, genuinely, that music was a form of worship. The rigour of counterpoint was not, for him, an obstacle to feeling but its vehicle. The discipline was the devotion.

This is perhaps the most important thing to understand about Bach: that his music resolves the apparent contradiction between intellectual structure and emotional depth. In most creative traditions, we assume these are in tension — that more structure means less feeling, that calculation is the enemy of spontaneity. Bach demolished this assumption. His most mathematically rigorous works — the fugues of the Well-Tempered Clavier, the final unfinished Art of Fugue — are also among the most emotionally devastating things in music.

The reason, I think, is that structure in Bach is not imposed from the outside but generated from the material itself. A Bach fugue does not feel like a mathematical object wearing an emotional disguise. It feels like a thought thinking itself — following its own logic to conclusions that are both surprising and, in retrospect, inevitable.

Why He Was Forgotten and Then Rediscovered

Bach died in 1750, and was almost immediately forgotten. The musical world moved on to the lighter, more accessible style of his son Carl Philipp Emanuel. For nearly eighty years, Bach's music was known only to specialists. Then, in 1829, the twenty-year-old Felix Mendelssohn conducted a performance of the St. Matthew Passion in Berlin — the first since Bach's death. The response was overwhelming. Within a decade, Bach's reputation had been rebuilt from near-nothing to what it has been ever since: the founding figure of Western music, the composer against whom all others are measured.

That rediscovery tells us something. When audiences heard Bach again in 1829, they were not hearing a historical curiosity. They were hearing something that sounded, despite being eighty years old, more advanced and more profound than almost anything being written at the time. That gap between historical distance and perpetual contemporaneity is the surest sign of a work's greatness. Bach is still ahead of us.
